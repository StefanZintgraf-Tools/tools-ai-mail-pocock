# AI-Mail — Foundation Vision

## Vision (press release)

### For individuals

- Important things handled or scheduled — you stop carrying email in your head.
- Noise shrinks itself instead of piling up.
- Nothing you needed gets silently lost.
- Costly fakes and scams caught before they cost you.
- Reads everything, does the legwork, taps you only when it matters.
- Spans your chat apps too, by choice — WhatsApp, Telegram and the rest in one
  calm place.
- Makes you warmer to people — not just less behind on them.
- Reach it however you like — speak or type, in any language.
- What it learns stays yours alone, and it earns your trust step by step.
- Nothing sent, decided, or deleted without your nod — you're always in charge.

### For businesses

- Client requests answered — nothing slips.
- Promising leads never cool off forgotten.
- Promised follow-ups actually go out.
- Repeat support questions answered fast, from how you solved them before.
- Years of email stay the company's — not gone when someone leaves.
- New hires brought up to speed from that memory.
- Money traps caught and questioned — forged invoice, changed bank details,
  urgent wire — before anyone's rushed into a costly mistake.
- Works *for* the company, never spying on staff.
- Every reply goes out in a real person's name.
- Nothing sent, decided, or deleted on its own — your people stay in charge.

## Use-cases

- **UC1.** As someone who never feels caught up, I can finally trust that every important
  message is either fully handled or scheduled to be — so I can close the laptop
  without it living rent-free in my head.
- **UC2.** As someone who doesn't want a robot running my life, I can finally have all the
  prep done for me — the reply drafted, the slot found, the decision laid out, some
  of it ready the moment it's needed and the rest on demand when I ask — while I stay
  the one who approves it, so nothing is ever sent or settled behind my back until
  I've confirmed it.
- **UC3.** As someone who mostly uses email for life-admin (bills, the kids' school,
  renewals, appointments, parcels), I can finally stop fearing the one dropped
  ball that costs money or time — the assistant catches the costly miss before it
  happens and asks me what to do.
- **UC4.** As someone who can never remember when things are due, I can finally rely on the
  assistant to know my deadlines and obligations on its own — I never have to
  enter a due date or tell it anything; it understood it from the email itself.
- **UC5.** As someone who treats my inbox like a junk drawer and knows the answer is buried
  somewhere, I can finally just ask in plain words and get it back — a quick fact
  ("what's my policy number?", "did I ever reply to the landlord?") or the exact
  buried message or thread ("the lawyer's note about the lease", "last year's quote
  from that supplier") — across everything from this week's pile to years of old
  mail, instead of digging or dreading the haystack.
- **UC6.** As someone who only realizes I needed something at the worst moment, I can
  finally have the assistant resurface the right thing on its own — the booking
  confirmation the morning I travel, the warranty when the appliance breaks —
  before I even think to look for it.
- **UC7.** As someone whose email is one giant undifferentiated pile, I can finally have it
  all neatly filed where it belongs — sorted into sensible places rather than
  heaped in one spot — so my correspondence is organized and safe, not a mess.
- **UC8.** As someone who loses track of important attachments, I can finally have the ones
  that matter saved off into a sensible, well-organized place of their own — so a
  contract, an invoice, or a photo is never trapped inside a buried email again.
- **UC9.** As someone who leaves the awkward emails unread for days, I can finally have the
  reply I dread already drafted in my own voice — the polite no, the third chase,
  the delicate client note — so the hardest ones take a tweak and a nod, not an
  afternoon of guilt.
- **UC10.** As someone who puts off the replies that take real legwork, I can finally have
  the answer assembled for me — the facts gathered, the numbers pulled together,
  the details lined up — so a reply that used to eat an hour is ready for me to
  check and approve.
- **UC11.** As someone who starts the day not knowing where to even begin, I can finally open
  to a short briefing — "here's the handful of things that actually need you today,
  everything else is handled" — instead of a bottomless reverse-chronological pile.
- **UC12.** As someone who can never fully hand the wheel to an AI, I can finally lean on the
  briefing while still being able to open and read everything myself whenever I
  want — the assistant earns my trust without ever locking me out of my own mail.
- **UC13.** As someone buried under newsletters, receipts, and notifications, I can finally
  have all that low-stakes noise set aside in its own quiet, read-only corner — not
  flagged as spam, just "for your information, nothing needed" — so it never clutters
  the mail that actually wants something from me.
- **UC14.** As someone whose friendships quietly drift because life gets busy, I can finally
  have the assistant notice when a thread with someone I care about is going cold —
  "you haven't replied to Anna in three weeks, want me to draft something?" — so I
  stay a good correspondent even when I don't have the time to be.
- **UC15.** As someone juggling important customers, I can finally be nudged before a key
  account or a promising new lead slips through the cracks — the follow-up that
  needed sending, the reply that's overdue — so relationships and revenue don't
  quietly cool off because I got busy.
- **UC16.** As someone who can't hold every detail of every relationship in my head, I can
  finally have the assistant remember the context across years — how we know each
  other, what we last spoke about, that their kid was sick — and draw on the full
  picture even when some of it lives outside my email, so I'm never caught blank
  and its nudges are never half-informed.
- **UC17.** As someone sitting on decades of accumulated email, I can finally turn that
  mountain into something useful — ask it questions and get answers out of years
  of correspondence — so the archive becomes a knowledge well instead of dead
  weight nobody dares touch.
- **UC18.** As someone dreading the wreckage of years of old mail, I can finally point the
  assistant at it and have it made safe and tidy — the genuinely dead stuff set
  aside, anything still valuable preserved and in order — so I go from "I can't
  even look at it" to "it's actually handled now."
- **UC19.** As someone afraid of losing something important in a big clear-out, I can finally
  trust that taming the old pile never loses what mattered — a still-valid warranty,
  an unanswered thread, an unclaimed refund gets surfaced rather than buried.
- **UC20.** As someone who could never spot the patterns across hundreds of emails, I can
  finally have the assistant stand watch over the whole picture — "you're paying
  for two storage plans", "this supplier crept up 30%", "three clients owe you
  £8k", "you promised the board a report and there's no draft" — catching things I
  never knew to ask about, and bringing them to me proactively in my briefing or a
  timely heads-up.
- **UC21.** As someone who loses days to scheduling back-and-forth, I can finally just say
  "set up a call with the Müllers next week" and have the whole negotiation happen
  in the background until there's a time everyone agreed — I only see the finished
  result to confirm, and the endless thread evaporates.
- **UC22.** As someone looking at a document I filed away, I can finally trace it back to the
  email it came from and see the whole correspondence around it — so I always know
  the story behind a file, not just the file.
- **UC23.** As someone trying to follow one topic, I can finally pull together every related
  message about it — even when it was handled by different people in separate
  threads — so I see the full back-and-forth of a subject as one coherent story
  instead of scattered fragments.
- **UC24.** As someone nervous about letting any software read my whole life, I can finally
  trust that what the assistant learns stays mine — it works for me alone, not for
  anyone watching over my shoulder, and I can always see what it knows, correct it,
  or switch it off and walk away with my mail intact.
- **UC25.** As someone with both a private inbox and a work inbox, I can finally set how
  careful the assistant is with each — my personal life and my business mail live
  by their own rules rather than one blanket setting, so each part of my life gets
  the privacy and the reach that actually fit it.
- **UC26.** As someone whose life is scattered across personal, work, and side-project
  addresses, I can finally have one calm assistant span all of them — no more
  switching apps and logins to feel on top of things — while it still keeps the
  walls I want, so a private thread never leaks into a work briefing.
- **UC27.** As someone who doesn't want to abandon the mail app I already live in, I can
  finally have the assistant come to me right where I work — help is one click away
  inside my existing inbox, not a whole new place I have to learn and move into.
- **UC28.** As someone who can't tell a clever fake from the real thing, I can finally have
  the assistant know what my normal looks like — my actual suppliers, contacts, and
  patterns — so it catches the forged invoice or the "we've changed our bank
  details" note that a plain spam filter would wave straight through.
- **UC29.** As someone who could be panicked into a costly mistake, I can finally have the
  assistant slow down the urgent-money traps — the boss-impersonation, the "pay this
  now" pressure, the wire-transfer scam — and warn me before any money moves, not
  after it's gone.
- **UC30.** As someone who worries about a less tech-savvy parent or relative, I can finally
  have the assistant quietly watch over their inbox too and raise a flag before they
  fall for something — protection that reaches the people I look out for.
- **UC31.** As someone who hates being treated like I can't think for myself, I can finally
  have the assistant explain in plain words why something smells wrong — so I learn
  to spot it myself and stay in charge, instead of just being told "blocked."
- **UC32.** As someone afraid that being protected means losing mail, I can finally trust that
  the assistant never deletes anything on its own — even a suspected scam is set
  aside and flagged for me to see, never quietly removed — so safety never costs me
  a message I might have wanted.
- **UC33.** As someone who wants to be thought of as warm and on-top-of-things, I can finally
  have the assistant help me be the person who never forgets a birthday, a thank-you,
  or a "how did the surgery go?" — so I come across as more thoughtful than I actually
  have time to be, and feel a little proud instead of guilty.
- **UC34.** As someone whose relationships are scattered across email and the chat apps I
  actually message people on, I can finally have one assistant that sees the whole
  picture of a person — so the birthday wish or the warm reply is drafted to go out the
  way I'd really send it, and a thread going cold gets noticed wherever it's quietly
  dying, not just in my inbox.
- **UC35.** As someone who wants email to stay my calm home base, I can finally bring my chats
  under the same assistant only if I choose to — all in one place if I like it, kept
  separate or left out entirely if I don't — so reach never gets forced on me and the
  one quiet place stays mine to define.
- **UC36.** As someone who stalls on the messages I have to start from scratch — the cold intro,
  the reconnection after years, the pitch to a stranger, the awkward ask — I can finally
  just say who and what, and have a real first draft assembled in my voice from what the
  assistant already knows about us both, so the blank page stops being the thing that
  makes me put it off.
- **UC37.** As someone who forgets the things I promised to send, I can finally have the assistant
  spot the loose ends on its own — "you said you'd send Tom your slides, want me to draft
  it?" — and offer to start the message before I've remembered I owe it; and if I find
  that too forward, I can simply turn that nudging down or off so it only ever drafts
  when I ask.
- **UC38.** As someone drowning in subscriptions I never chose to keep, I can finally have the
  assistant notice what I never actually read and offer to unsubscribe me — quietly
  shrinking the firehose at the source instead of just sweeping it aside — so over time
  there's simply less arriving to deal with, and never a list pruned silently: it always
  shows me what it wants to cut and waits for my nod.
- **UC39.** As someone who finds email stressful and never trusted myself with it, I can finally
  have a patient helper at my shoulder that speaks plainly and lets me just ask — "is
  this one real?", "what does this letter want?" — so the inbox stops being a place I'm
  afraid to get wrong, and I can handle my own mail with confidence instead of having to
  call someone every time.
- **UC40.** As someone who'd rather just say what I need than hunt through menus, I can finally
  reach my whole mailbox by talking or typing in plain language — speak it when my hands
  are full or screens intimidate me, type it when I want it quiet — so the easiest way in
  is always open, whoever I am and whatever the moment.
- **UC41.** As someone uneasy about handing my whole private inbox to something new, I can finally
  start small and watch it earn my trust — it shows its thinking, gets the easy things
  right first, and widens what it handles only as I let it — so I'm never asked for a
  blind leap of faith, just a first careful step.
- **UC42.** As someone whose correspondence spans several languages, I can finally have the
  assistant meet each message in its own tongue — reading, drafting, and replying in the
  sender's language while I keep the app itself in the one language I'm comfortable in —
  so a French supplier or a German client never means switching tools or fumbling a reply.
- **UC43.** As someone fielding the same support questions over and over, I can finally have the
  assistant draft each answer from how we solved it before — checking past mail first,
  then our own knowledge base when the answer isn't there — so customers get a fast,
  accurate reply and my team stops rewriting the same explanation, with a person still
  approving every send.
- **UC44.** As the person who sorts our shared inbox into everyone's folders by hand, I can
  finally hand that daily chore to the assistant — it learns how our team already splits the
  work and files each message into the right person's place the way I would, so I get my
  morning back instead of being the human sorting machine; and when it isn't sure, it leaves
  the message in the common pile rather than guess, because a wrong filing is worse than an
  unfiled one — anyone can re-assign, and it learns from the correction.
- **UC45.** As someone on a team where mail used to rot in the folder of whoever happened to be
  away, I can finally trust the assistant to know who's actually reachable — on holiday, off
  sick, away on a trip — so it never drops a time-sensitive message on someone who can't see
  it, steering it to whoever's covering instead, and the shared inbox keeps flowing even when
  the usual sorter is the one who's out.
- **UC46.** As one of several people working out of the same inbox, I can finally see when a
  colleague is already on a message right now — before I start writing my own reply — so the
  customer never gets two answers (or two *different* answers) and none of us waste time
  doubling up on something that's already in hand.
- **UC47.** As someone who could never quite trust software that touches my private mail, I can
  finally ask it to show me everything it did and why — filed these, drafted those, set this
  aside as suspicious, unsubscribed me from that — each with a plain-words reason, so it's
  never a black box: I trust it precisely because nothing it does is hidden from me.
- **UC48.** As someone answerable for how our company's inbox is handled, I can finally rely on a
  complete, lasting record of every decision the assistant made — so any single message can be
  traced back and accounted for long after the fact, and an audit holds no fear — while that
  record stays an account of the *assistant's own* actions, never a way to watch over what my
  staff were doing.
- **UC49.** As someone passing a conversation to a colleague — or inheriting one — I can finally
  have it arrive already understood: the whole story in plain words (what they want, what's
  been promised, what's still open, and why it's landing with me) travels *with* the message,
  so nothing falls through the crack between us and whoever picks it up loses no time
  reconstructing a cold "fwd:" from the bottom up.
- **UC50.** As someone who hits a message that's beyond me — a technical question, a legal nuance,
  the thing that really needs the boss — I can finally have the assistant point it to the right
  specialist and hand it over with the full picture, so the customer reaches the person who can
  actually answer instead of being bounced around, and escalating is one nod rather than a hunt
  for who deals with this.
